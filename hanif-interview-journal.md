# Hanif Carroll — Interview Journal
*A personal document capturing beliefs, motivations, and philosophy. June 2026.*

---

## On AI being fundamentally different

I first started to realize that AI was fundamentally different when it evolved past being used only for autocomplete in code. When I saw that it was able to allow someone to create an application — essentially vibe-code something into existence that was actually useful — I knew it was going to change things in a real way.

I have a very practical point of view when it comes to life. I'm all about having tools that help you get things done. And I saw firsthand how AI was helping me build all sorts of things I wouldn't even attempt in the past, because the effort would have just been too much.

---

## On building Casamo

I'm building my first real SaaS product now, called Casamo. It's a tool for digital nomads to help them find a good long-term stay for their trip — somewhere they can actually work from.

Without AI, it's easy to understand why there are different types of engineers — front end, back end, and so on. But with AI, I'm able to handle all of it. I can create a complete application starting with just an idea, put it on the internet, and have the possibility to earn money from it.

It started out as something for my own purposes — an app that searched Airbnb listings and found ones that had a washing machine inside the apartment, as opposed to a shared one in the building. I was a digital nomad for six months before I moved full time to Buenos Aires, and I still stay in short-term apartments, so the problem still affects me. There's nothing comfortable about staying somewhere that isn't set up for a good stay — if the place is noisy, or the kitchen is missing basic things like pots and pans.

This is really about me practicing being a builder. Noticing a problem, thinking I can create the solution, thinking it might be useful to other people, and trying to see if that's true. It's not a passionate bet on the product idea itself — it's me learning what it means to build something for someone else.

---

## On the pressure that comes with possibility

It feels empowering to have the ability to build pretty much anything you want. But it puts a lot of pressure on you too, because it can feel like you're wasting your time when you don't know what you want just yet.

I don't entirely know where that feeling comes from, but I think it's that I sometimes wait for a signal from the outside world before deciding what to go all in on. It's not for lack of wanting — for years now I've wanted to have my own startup that I could dedicate my time and energy to. Something I could grow over the years and grow with. Something I could eventually earn a living from.

I'm very inspired by successful indie hackers who start with zero MRR and are able to get to thousands and tens of thousands of dollars per month.

---

## On the lesson I learned the hard way

About two and a half years ago, I quit my job and started freelancing — and that was not the right thing to do. I should have started freelancing while I still had the job, so I could build it up with some stability before switching completely. The sequencing matters as much as the ambition.

That's why I'm looking for a job right now. I still want the stability for a while — so I can build properly, without that pressure of survival distorting every decision.

---

## On what I'm looking for in a role

I've always had trouble recognizing my own skills and talking about myself as someone talented. But now I realize that I am a talented, thoughtful, and intelligent builder — and that any team would be lucky to have me as a member.

I'm looking for a job where I can use AI to solve real problems. It needs to be a position where I have autonomy. I'm not interested in being a ticket monkey.

More specifically, I'm passionate about talking to users and customers, understanding their problems, and then figuring out how I can use AI — and other technology and engineering techniques — to solve those problems. That intersection of customer empathy and technical execution is where I do my best work.

---

## On quality versus speed

Quality versus speed depends on the scope of the project. I'm obviously more willing to accept risk and move quickly on a personal project than I would be on a shared codebase with a team.

But either way, I believe in guardrails. Just because you use AI doesn't mean you have to sacrifice safety and correctness. Automated tests and linters are more important than ever. Automated QA is a good idea. There are ways to ship code safely even when you're moving fast — and AI actually makes it easier to have those guardrails, not harder.

---

## On the hardest part of building a product

The hardest part isn't the code. Coding is the comfortable part — it's what every builder is familiar with. But it's not what gets you users. They won't come just because you built something. You still have to get people to know the product exists and understand what it does.

I'm planning to start a private beta soon. After that, I'll have to learn how to market it. I don't have a fully formed answer for that yet — but I'll figure it out once I get solid feedback from real users.

---

## On Buenos Aires and what it's taught me

Living in Buenos Aires has strengthened the idea that technology is meant to make people's lives better. There's a very human component to it. The culture here is much more social, and I'm naturally drawn to technology — so the two things combine in a way that feels right. It reminds me that the product is never the point. The person using it is.

---

## On where AI takes us

It's really hard to predict what's going to happen in 10 years with AI, because even three years ago feels like an eternity. The difference in model capabilities is shocking. And even if models stopped improving tomorrow, we would still get better at using them. If AI keeps accelerating the way it has been, I don't think we can really conceive of what that world would look like.

But I don't think the most important skill is taste — that's become a meme, and honestly I find it a bit annoying. I think the most important skill is, always has been, and will continue to be: **knowing what you want**. It's about being intentional with your life.

If you don't know what you want, you can never reach your goals. And knowing what you want requires real introspection — understanding yourself by noticing how you react to certain things. What makes your heart race with excitement. What kind of tasks make you dread the day ahead. That self-knowledge is the foundation of everything.

---

## On what keeps me going

This is just what I'm drawn to do. There's nothing else I could do. If I were doing anything else, I would feel unfulfilled.

---

## On what I want for the future

In three to five years, the ideal future is one where I have a product that earns me more than enough money to live and save comfortably — and I'm able to use my extra time to help other people build things, or learn how to build things.

It would probably be something local and community-oriented. Creating an indie hacker community here in Buenos Aires. Teaching people how to build products. Setting up a space where people can socialize and help each other.

I like the idea of making people happy and making their lives easier with the product I build. And on the flip side, I love the idea of empowering people to reach their goals and become better versions of themselves. I love technology. If I can mix all of those things, that would be perfect.

---

## The one thing

I'm an empathetic builder that uses technology to empower people.
