<CaseSection title="Summary">
	HablaBA started from a local language-learning problem: most exchange products feel like profile browsing, dating apps, or large events where meaningful conversation is hard.

    I turned the idea into a working web and mobile product around small-group practice, neighborhood discovery, and real-world coordination.

</CaseSection>

<CaseSection title="Business Objective">
	The product needed to make practice feel more intentional than swiping through profiles while still being lightweight enough for people to actually use.

    That meant focusing the MVP on groups, discovery, chat, and coordination instead of trying to become a full social network from day one.

</CaseSection>

<CaseSection title="The Work">
	<DecisionBlock title="Centered the product on practice, not profiles.">
		The experience prioritized small group exchanges and neighborhood-based discovery so learners could find real opportunities to practice.
	</DecisionBlock>

    <DecisionBlock title="Kept web and mobile connected to one backend.">
    	The product used shared backend foundations so mobile coordination and web discovery could support the same usage pattern without splitting into separate products.
    </DecisionBlock>

</CaseSection>

<CaseSection title="Product Proof" media>
	The MVP shows how trust-heavy local products need product shape, coordination, and launch constraints to work together.

    <ImagePlate image={hablabaFeature} alt="HablaBA language exchange product interface." />

</CaseSection>

<CaseSection title="Results">
	The project became a working product with onboarding, group chat, neighborhood discovery, and cross-platform coordination.

    It remains a useful proof point for how I approach MVPs where user behavior and product trust matter as much as the code.

</CaseSection>
